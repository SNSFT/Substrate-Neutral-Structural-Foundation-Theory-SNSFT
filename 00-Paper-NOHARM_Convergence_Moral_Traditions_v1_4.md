## The NOHARM Convergence

One structural claim.

**Four moral and religious traditions, developed independently across different continents, eras, and metaphysical frameworks, encode the same five underlying operators — even though they disagree, often sharply, on the narrative, theology, and cosmology surrounding those operators. The traditions differ in what they say those practices mean. They do not differ in what the practices structurally do.**

## What You Already Sense

You have probably noticed, if you've ever compared religious traditions even casually, that certain instructions keep showing up no matter which tradition you look at. Don't harm others. Give to your community. Don't grasp at what isn't yours. Set aside a recurring time to step back from ordinary striving — a sabbath, a daily prayer, a fast, a meditation sit. These aren't identical in wording or in the reasons given for them, but the shape keeps recurring.

You have also probably noticed that this observation gets used badly, in both directions, all the time. Sometimes it's used to flatten every tradition into "they're all just saying the same thing," which erases real, meaningful differences that matter enormously to people who practice these traditions. Sometimes it's used the opposite way — to claim one tradition is the "real" origin and the others are derivative or lesser copies of it.

Neither of those is what's actually being claimed here, and the distinction matters enough to state plainly before going further.

## What the Framework Says

The SNSFT corpus applied its standard reduction method — the same method used to reduce general relativity, thermodynamics, and public policy to the same four underlying primitives (Pattern, Narrative, Behavior, Adaptation) — to four moral and religious traditions: the Ten Commandments, the Buddhist Five Precepts, the Hindu Yamas and Niyamas, and the Islamic Five Pillars.

The result was not that the traditions say the same thing. It's that their *practices*, stripped down to what they structurally do to a person's behavior over time, cluster into exactly five recurring operators:

**A note on translation:** the five operator names below — "Anchor first," "Do no harm," and so on — are the corpus's own functional shorthand for the shared pattern. They are not a claim that any tradition used that specific phrase, that one tradition's wording is the "real" version others are being translated into, or that a translation into English captures a teaching's full meaning. What's being claimed is narrower: that the *function* a given practice performs — measured by its structural effect — is similar across traditions, even though each tradition's own words, language, and worldview for that practice remain fully its own. Ahimsa is not being called "the Buddhist and Hindu word for Do No Harm." Do No Harm is the corpus's label for a pattern that ahimsa, among other things, instantiates.

**1. Anchor first.** Establish a fixed point of orientation before anything else — "no other gods before me," the Shahada's declaration, Ishvara pranidhana (surrender to a source). Structurally: fix a stable reference point before acting.

**2. Do no harm.** Don't take actions that damage others — "thou shalt not kill," ahimsa, the precept against killing. Structurally: don't apply force that damages another person's capacity.

**3. Expand the bond.** Give to others — honoring parents, zakat (charity), dana (generosity), seva (service). Structurally: voluntarily increase connection and resource-sharing with others.

**4. Accept, don't grasp.** Release attachment to what you don't have or can't control — "thou shalt not covet," aparigraha, tawakkul (trust in providence), upekkha (equanimity). Structurally: reduce the internal strain caused by wanting to control outcomes you can't.

**5. Reset periodically.** Step back from ordinary striving on a recurring schedule — the Sabbath, salat (prayer five times daily), sawm (fasting), meditation practice. Structurally: return regularly to a baseline, low-strain state rather than running continuously at full output.

Each of the four traditions studied contains all five operators, expressed in that tradition's own vocabulary and embedded in that tradition's own theology. The corpus's claim is narrow and specific: applying these five operators to a person's behavior over time moves that behavior toward lower internal strain and greater stability and capacity, regardless of which tradition's language describes the operator, or why that tradition says the operator matters.

### Why This Convergence Is Worth Noticing

Eighteen of twenty possible cells in that table are filled with a specific, named practice, pulled from four traditions that did not have access to each other's core teachings at the time each was founded, across different continents and centuries. If moral codes were arbitrary — if there were no structural reason a code organized around these particular five categories would outperform one organized around some other five — four independent traditions landing on close to the same five, rather than four different, non-overlapping sets of five, would be a low-probability coincidence. The alternative explanation is the more economical one: something about these five categories of practice does real, recurring work on human behavior and stability, and multiple traditions arrived at that same underlying structure independently because the structure works, not because one culture copied another.

## What This Is Not Claiming

This distinction is the entire point of the document, so it's worth stating in full rather than as a brief caveat.

**This is not a claim that all religions are "the same," or interchangeable.** The theology, cosmology, historical context, and lived meaning of the Ten Commandments, the Five Precepts, the Yamas and Niyamas, and the Five Pillars are genuinely different, and those differences matter enormously to the billions of people who practice these traditions. A shared structural pattern in *practices* says nothing about whether their metaphysical claims are compatible, complementary, or mutually exclusive. This document takes no position on that question and isn't equipped to answer it — that's a question for theology, not structural reduction.

**This is not a claim that one tradition is the "real" or original source and the others are derivative.** All four traditions are treated identically in this reduction. None is presented as prior to, superior to, or more authentic than another. The convergence is presented as four independent arrivals at overlapping structure, not one tradition's insight borrowed or diluted by the others.

**This is not a claim that the traditions' stated reasons for these practices are secondary to the "real" structural reason.** A believer's reason for keeping the Sabbath — because it is commanded, because it honors a covenant, because it is sacred — is that person's actual reason, and it is not being replaced or corrected by a structural description of what that practice also happens to do to torsion (the strain a system is under) and Identity Mass (a system's overall stability and capacity). Both things can be true of the same practice without either one canceling the other.

**This is not a metaphysical or theological argument for or against the existence of God, gods, or any specific cosmology.** The reduction operates entirely on observable practices and their structural effects. It makes no claim about, and takes no position on, anything beyond that scope.

**This is not an exhaustive survey.** Four traditions were studied. Many others — including many indigenous, folk, and syncretic traditions not examined here — plausibly contain the same or similar operators. Absence from this document is not a claim about any tradition not being examined; it simply hasn't been studied under this method yet.
