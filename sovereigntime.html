01 · THE SUBSTRATE-NEUTRAL RATIO
Every atomic clock has a torsion ratio τ = B / P
B is the transition frequency the clock actually observes. P is the pattern rigidity — for heavy-nucleus hyperfine clocks this is the neutron count. For Cs-133, P = 78. For Rb-87, P = 50. Dividing the observed frequency by pattern gives τ, the substrate's structural torsion.
02 · THE TORSION LIMIT
The phase boundary TL = SAC / 10
Any substrate with τ < TL sits in the Locked phase and is a stable atomic reference. The deeper below TL, the wider the stability margin. Cs at τ = 0.118 sits deep. Rb-87 at τ = 0.137 sits at the edge. That is why Cs is primary and Rb is secondary.
03 · WHY SR-87 IS DIFFERENT
Optical lattice clocks reach Noble (τ = 0)
Strontium-87 traps thousands of identical atoms in a lattice. Under 8-beam PSY fusion of identical atoms, B_out = max(0, 8B − 56B) = 0. The lattice collectively drives to Noble phase — deeper than any hyperfine clock. This is why optical lattice clocks achieve 10⁻¹⁸ fractional uncertainty.
04 · WHY HYDROGEN NEEDS FUSION
H-1 requires higher-order PNBA fusion
Under simple τ = B/P, H-1 sits above TL. But H-1's adaptation coupling A = g_e·g_p/4 ≈ 2.796 carries real weight. Full fusion τ = B/(P·N·A) puts H-1 at 0.464 × TL — moderately locked. The framework detects which substrates need higher-order fusion by their A axis strength.
