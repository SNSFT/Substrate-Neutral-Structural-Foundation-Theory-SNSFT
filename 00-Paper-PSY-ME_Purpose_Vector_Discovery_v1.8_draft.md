## §6 · Sources of Purpose Vector Interruption

The framework identifies six primary sources through which purpose vector discovery is interrupted in modern developmental environments. These are documented for parent awareness, not for blame — most sources of interruption are well-intentioned and reflect adult judgment operating from within cultural frames that do not include the ME framework.

**Source 1 — Well-meaning adult redirection.** Parents, teachers, and extended family who observe a child's deep interest and interpret it as unhealthy fixation, social isolation, or missed opportunity for broader development. Well-meaning intervention typically takes the form of scheduling other activities, discouraging depth in favor of breadth, or redirecting attention toward "socially appropriate" interests.

**Source 2 — Cultural pressure toward well-roundedness.** Modern educational and cultural systems reward broad competence across multiple domains rather than deep expertise in one. College admissions, résumé construction, extracurricular expectations, and standardized testing all prioritize breadth. The child who wants to go deep on one thing is systematically disadvantaged by systems that require breadth.

**Source 3 — Clinical management of "fixation."** Some clinical frameworks treat deep sustained interest as pathological when it is intense, exclusive, or focused on domains adults find unusual. Management typically involves reducing the interest's intensity or duration, redirecting the interest to more "functional" domains, or medicating the behaviors associated with sustained focus.

**Source 4 — Educational insistence on curriculum coverage.** Standardized curricula require coverage of specific subjects at specific times. A child whose purpose vector discovery is running through one subject cannot spend the necessary depth of time on it because the curriculum demands they move to other subjects. Even AP, honors, and gifted tracks generally require breadth rather than depth.

**Source 5 — Algorithmic content environments.** Social media, streaming platforms, and algorithmic recommendation systems are optimized for constant novelty and attention capture. A child whose developmental task requires sustained depth on one substrate is instead exposed to environments that reward and reinforce constant substrate-switching. The developmental cost is not caused by malice but by mismatched optimization.

**Source 6 — Economic pressure toward employability.** Adolescents and young adults face economic pressure to develop skills for the job market. Purpose vector discovery often runs through domains that do not have obvious economic payoff. Adult authority figures may redirect the young person toward more employable directions, interrupting a vector that would have consolidated into something economically valuable in its own way but on a longer timescale than short-term employability requires.

**None of these sources is malicious.** Each represents adult judgment operating from within cultural frames that do not include the ME framework's account of purpose vector as developmental mechanism. Making the framework's account explicit is the intervention this paper offers.

## §7 · What Parents Can Actually Do

The framework's actionable claim for parents is straightforward:

**Protect the purpose vector discovery process. Do not interrupt it. When it looks obsessive, that is not a signal to redirect. It is a signal that the discovery process is running and needs protection.**

Specific parent actions the framework recommends:

**Protect depth.** When your child is deep in a subject, do not interrupt for well-roundedness. Do not schedule other activities during their vector time. Do not redirect them toward more "balanced" interests. The depth is the developmental process running.

**Provide substrate.** Whatever the interest is, provide access to the substrate the child needs to go deeper. Books, materials, tools, mentors, access to communities of practice. Substrate is what builds the PCorpus the child's A-axis GPU will eventually integrate against.

**Advocate against interruption sources.** School systems, extended family members, and cultural pressures may push toward well-roundedness. Advocate for your child's depth. Frame the depth in framework terms when possible — "this is her purpose vector consolidating" is a much stronger position than "she really likes rocks."

**Delay employability optimization.** Purpose vector consolidation happens on longer timescales than short-term economic thinking. Protect the vector through late adolescence and early adulthood when possible. Vectors that consolidate produce economic value on their own timescale, often exceeding what redirection to conventional employment would have produced.

**Trust the child's direction.** ME children generally know what their vector is even when they cannot articulate it. If your child persistently returns to a specific substrate across years, that is the vector. Trust it even when it seems impractical.

### 7.1 · For Older Children and Adults Whose Vector Discovery Was Interrupted

The framework-identified intervention for adolescents and adults whose vector discovery did not complete during childhood is **PCorpus building through substrate deepening**: sustained engagement with a single domain over years, at a depth sufficient to build the P-axis PCorpus that matches existing A-axis GPU output. This intervention is simple to state and structurally difficult to execute, and the difficulty is worth naming honestly.

**The specific mechanism the intervention has to address:**

Purpose vector discovery in early childhood normally involves a **closed-loop reward system**. The engagement with the substrate becomes intrinsically rewarding as PCorpus builds — pattern deepening, structure emerging, and connections consolidating produce their own confirmation. External validation may exist but is not the primary reinforcement. PCorpus accumulates because the substrate itself is the reward, which lets the GPU-PCorpus match consolidate over time. Historical exemplars in [9,9,3,44] all show this pattern: Shelley's engagement with literature and Enlightenment thought was intrinsically rewarding; Bruce Lee's engagement with martial arts systems was intrinsically rewarding; Musashi's engagement with the sword was intrinsically rewarding. External recognition came later and did not drive the engagement.

When vector discovery is interrupted early enough — through the invalidation-to-NCorpus mechanism documented in §5.4, or when the developmental environment (particularly modern algorithmic content environments) trains the child's reward system to fire against external validation before internal reward has consolidated — a different reward pattern can establish itself. Call this **externalized reward calibration**. In this configuration, engagement with any substrate is sustained only as long as external validation continues (views, likes, praise, financial return, social recognition). When the external signal drops, engagement decays and the person shifts to a different substrate seeking the reward signal back. PCorpus never accumulates deeply on any single substrate because the reward system prevents sustained engagement long enough for the accumulation to complete.

The externalized reward pattern is architecturally distinct from vector interruption alone. Vector interruption by external adults produces an ME identity that WOULD build PCorpus if permitted. Externalized reward calibration produces an ME identity whose own internal reward system pulls them off any substrate before PCorpus buildup can reach match-depth with GPU output. The person is not being interrupted from outside anymore; they are interrupting themselves from inside — carrying forward the NCorpus of childhood invalidation as an internal signal that unrewarded effort is unsafe.

**The observable pattern of externalized reward calibration:**

- Attempts many substrates without settling on one
- Progresses to competent (but not expert) level on each before shifting to another
- Frames the shifting as "trying to find what fits" or "figuring out what I want to do"
- Reports feeling behind, unfocused, or "good at lots of things but great at nothing"
- Experiences strong initial engagement with new substrates that decays as external validation plateaus
- Often correlates with heavy engagement in social media or algorithmic content environments
- Cannot easily identify a domain that feels intrinsically rewarding without external confirmation

The population presenting this pattern overlaps significantly with AuDHD self-identifiers and with adults who describe themselves as "high-potential but underachieving," "multi-passionate," or "polymath types who can't commit." Framework-level, the pattern reads as A-dominant HRIS with high GPU output, insufficient PCorpus to integrate it, and externalized reward calibration preventing PCorpus buildup — a distinct variant from the P-dominant savant configurations documented at [9,9,7,1], where PCorpus buildup is prevented by different mechanisms (typically N-axis suppression rather than reward calibration).

**What PCorpus building requires in this configuration:**

PCorpus building through substrate deepening is not sufficient on its own when reward calibration is externalized. The person can select a substrate and start going deep on it, but their existing reward system will pull them off it before PCorpus has time to accumulate to match-depth. The intervention has to include a specific structural component: **sustained engagement with a substrate specifically without external validation, for a period long enough that internal reward can develop.** This is the hard part.

Concretely, this means engaging with a domain during a period of no external return — no views, no likes, no money, no recognition, no signal from anyone that the engagement matters. During this period, the existing externalized reward system will register the engagement as unrewarding and will pull the person toward substrates that promise faster external validation. The intervention requires holding through this period until PCorpus accumulates to the point where the substrate itself becomes internally rewarding, which typically takes months to years depending on the depth of prior externalized calibration and the NCorpus load carried from childhood invalidation.

**The framework does not claim this is easy or fast.** For an ME identity who has spent years or decades operating with externalized reward calibration, the shift toward internal reward requires deliberate structural work that most environments actively work against. Social media environments, gig economy structures, engagement-driven cultural systems, and short-form content platforms are all optimized to reinforce externalized reward calibration. A person attempting PCorpus building under these conditions has to build environmental protection they may not have received in childhood — deliberately limiting exposure to fast external validation cycles, deliberately choosing substrates that will not produce visible output for extended periods, and deliberately tolerating the discomfort of engagement that feels unrewarded because the internal reward has not yet consolidated.

**Practical structural moves the framework identifies:**

- **Select a substrate that cannot be easily performed publicly.** Domains that require long private PCorpus accumulation before any output exists (a technical specialty, a craft tradition, a body of formal knowledge) protect the person from their own externalized reward system because there is nothing to publish yet.
- **Deliberately restrict external validation channels during PCorpus accumulation.** This is not a lifetime commitment; it is a period of protection long enough that internal reward can consolidate. Once PCorpus has reached match-depth with GPU output, external validation can be reintegrated without the substrate switching pattern reasserting.
- **Track internal reward emergence as a distinct signal.** The consolidation of internal reward feels like the substrate becoming its own reason. Once this signal appears, it can be trusted as evidence that PCorpus is accumulating and beginning to match GPU output.
- **Accept that the first six to eighteen months will feel unrewarding.** This is the period during which PCorpus is building and has not yet reached match-depth. The existing externalized reward system will interpret this period as "this substrate isn't working" and try to pull the person off. Recognizing this pattern as expected reduces the likelihood of interruption.
- **Do not attempt PCorpus building while under acute external validation pressure.** Financial precarity, relationship instability, career pressure, and social status stress all activate the externalized reward system and make PCorpus building harder. Structural protection of the PCorpus-building period is part of the intervention.

**The framework's actionable claim for adults in this configuration is: PCorpus building is available at any age, but the environmental conditions required to complete it are structurally distinct from those required by children whose vector discovery is still open.** Adults have to build the protection themselves, which is difficult but not impossible, and the historical exemplars in [9,9,3,44] demonstrate that consolidated vectors produce output that is worth the difficulty of the accumulation process.

This intervention is a developmental direction, not a treatment. It is not a substitute for professional support if mental health, executive function difficulties, or life circumstances warrant clinical or coaching intervention. See §9 (MRC) for full boundary.
