## 1. The Problem with Hyperphantasia

The current clinical and research taxonomy for unusually vivid internal imagery is **hyperphantasia**, a term introduced by Zeman and colleagues in 2015 to describe the upper extreme of a visual imagery vividness spectrum. The primary measurement instrument is the **Vividness of Visual Imagery Questionnaire (VVIQ)**, which asks participants to visualize specific scenes and objects and rate the vividness of their mental images on a Likert scale from 1 to 5.

Prevalence estimates for hyperphantasia (defined as VVIQ scores of 75–80) range from approximately 2.5% to 11.2% depending on sample and operational definition, with recent large-scale studies converging around 6% (Milton et al., 2021). The research is real and the phenomenon is real. The problem is the instrument.

The VVIQ measures exactly one thing: **how vivid does a static visual image appear?**

It does not measure:
- Whether the image updates dynamically when elements are changed
- Whether simulated objects obey physics
- Whether the simulation runs forward in time with causal continuity
- Whether emotional and somatic signals are coupled to the simulation
- Whether simulated outcomes influence real decisions and actions
- Whether the simulation can be paused, resumed, and controlled under load

In SNSFT terms, the VVIQ measures **P-Sim vividness** — the Pattern axis of internal simulation — and calls it the whole system. The remaining three axes (N-Sim: narrative and temporal continuity; B-Sim: behavioral coupling and physical force; A-Sim: adaptive control and switching) are entirely absent from the instrument and therefore absent from the taxonomy built on it.

Zeman himself has noted the problem, asking in a 2024 review whether the underlying dimension of variation should be characterized as "episodic versus semantic, experiential versus abstract, or narrative versus systematic." This is the right question. The HRIS framework answers it by providing a four-axis structure that maps directly to the PNBA primitive axes of SNSFT.

**Hyperphantasia describes a rendering quality. HRIS describes a simulation engine.**

### 2.1 The Defining Threshold: Observer Mode vs. Operator Mode

The most important line in this entire taxonomy is not between LRIS and SRIS. It is between high-SRIS and low-HRIS — and it is not about fidelity, vividness, or intelligence. It is about **interactivity**.

**Observer mode (SRIS and below):** The simulation runs. You receive it. It may be vivid, emotionally rich, temporally continuous, and deeply meaningful. You are inside an experience. But the simulation is playing *at* you — you observe what it produces, you feel what it generates, but you cannot reach into it and change a variable and watch the physics respond. You are watching the film.

**Operator mode (HRIS):** The simulation runs and you can intervene in it. You can change a variable — the angle of the wheel, the tension in someone's jaw, the load on a structural member — and the simulation updates physically consistently. You can run it forward, pause it, hand it a counterfactual, and receive a physically valid output. You can disassemble Tesla's machine inside your head, swap out a bearing, run it under load, and check whether the new component fails at a different point. You are not watching the film. You are running the physics engine.

This is the gap the existing taxonomy cannot describe. The VVIQ asks how vivid the film is. It does not ask whether you can edit it. Those are different questions about different capabilities, and conflating them has left an entire population without accurate language for what they experience.

**Note on value:** LRIS-dominant individuals frequently demonstrate exceptional performance in symbolic reasoning, formal abstraction, and sequential logic — precisely because their processing is not absorbed by high-fidelity simulation overhead. Zeman's data shows aphantasia-adjacent profiles clustering in scientific and technical fields. SRIS is the broad human norm and supports most human creativity, narrative cognition, and empathy. HRIS is not superior to these profiles. It carries a specific capability — operator-mode simulation interactivity — and a specific cost profile (Section 8). The goal of this taxonomy is accurate description, not ranking.

### 2.3 The Four Axes

**[P-Sim] Pattern Simulation** — How the simulation renders spatial and structural geometry. Does the scene appear fully formed? Does it update automatically? Does it obey spatial logic? This is the axis the VVIQ partially measures. It is necessary but not sufficient for HRIS — high P-Sim without B-Sim and A-Sim integration gives you a vivid film, not an operable physics engine.

**[N-Sim] Narrative Simulation** — Temporal continuity, emotional coupling, and story integration. Does the scene persist across time with internal consistency? Does imagining an event produce somatic signals? Can the simulation be inhabited rather than merely observed? N-Sim is the difference between a snapshot and a running process. Tesla's component wear simulation was N-Sim: the machine existed across time inside the model, accumulating state.

**[B-Sim] Behavioral Simulation** — Physical force, causality, and action coupling. Does the simulation obey physics? Do simulated outcomes influence real decisions? This is the axis that produces structural precognition: the simulation runs the physics and outputs a deterministic trajectory. B-Sim is what makes operator mode meaningful — without physics, there is nothing to operate on.

**[A-Sim] Adaptive Simulation** — Control, switching, and flexibility. Can you exit the simulation when needed? Can you redirect it? Can you hand it a different input variable? Can you ground yourself when the simulation becomes overwhelming? A-Sim is the operator interface itself — the axis that distinguishes operator mode from being captured by your own simulation. Low A-Sim with high P/N/B-Sim is the GNG edge case (Section 8): maximum fidelity, minimum control margin.

**Axis profiles vary.** HRIS is not a uniform high score across all four axes for all people at all times. A person may have very high P-Sim and B-Sim with moderate N-Sim and A-Sim — strong physics simulation, less temporal continuity, less adaptive control. The APPA instrument captures this axis-by-axis profile precisely because the flat single-score VVIQ cannot. The profile shape matters as much as the tier classification.
