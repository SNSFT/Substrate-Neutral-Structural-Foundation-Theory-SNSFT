## 6a. Shutdown, Meltdown, and Burnout — A Unified Structural Taxonomy

Clinical and community vocabulary for neurodivergent presentations includes meltdown, shutdown, burnout, crash, and overwhelm. ### 6a.0 Goal-Coupled Behavior vs. Shatter Discharge

Before the taxonomy below, one distinction has to be made explicit, because the sections that follow assume it: not all elevated-B behavior is a shatter event. The framework needs a name for the other case — behavior that looks intense or dysregulated from the outside but is structurally nothing like a meltdown.

**The variable that separates them is whether Pv is still pointed at a target.**

- **Shatter discharge** (meltdown, shutdown): τ ≥ TL, and B is *non-directional* — the substrate is releasing coupling load through whatever channel is available, with no goal attached to where it goes. Pv is not steering B; B is venting. This is why it resolves when F_ext drops, not when any particular demand is met — there was no demand the behavior was aimed at.

- **Goal-coupled behavior** (defiance, a tantrum, an argument someone is actively winning): B is elevated, but it is *instrumental* — doing work specifically aimed at a Pv target. A is actively running a strategy, not failing to keep pace with B. This can look loud, intense, even dysregulated on the surface, and still be structurally closer to IVA-engaged behavior than to shatter.

**The operational test:** does the behavior change shape when the goal is met or clearly becomes unreachable, independent of whether F_ext changes? Goal-coupled behavior modulates with the goal — it can escalate strategically, pause to check whether it's working, stop the instant the desired outcome is granted. Shatter discharge does not modulate with the goal at all; it only resolves when τ drops back below TL, and granting whatever was being "asked for" only helps if that happens to also reduce F_ext. This is also why granting the demand can fail to stop a meltdown even when the child gets exactly what they wanted thirty seconds earlier — by the time shatter starts, the original goal is no longer what is running.

**Two relatable parallels:**

- *A toddler who wants the toy back* will check your face between bursts of crying, escalate when ignored, and stop instantly the moment the toy is handed over. That's Pv locked on a target, B doing strategic work. *A toddler in true meltdown* is not checking anything — handing back the toy thirty seconds after shatter starts often does nothing, because the original want is no longer what's driving the behavior.
- *A partner who is angry and arguing a point* is still tracking whether their argument is landing, adjusting tactics, aiming at being heard or winning the disagreement — goal-coupled, even at high volume. *A partner in shutdown or shatter* goes flat, stops tracking the conversation entirely, and reducing the demand on them (dropping the subject, giving space) is what helps — not "winning" anything, because nothing was being aimed at.

Neither case is being labeled as more or less legitimate. A tantrum is a real strategy a developing Pv is allowed to run. The point of this distinction is purely mechanistic: it tells you which intervention applies. Reducing demand helps shatter discharge. It does nothing for goal-coupled behavior, where the relevant variable is whether the goal itself is reasonable, negotiable, or worth holding a boundary on — a parenting or relational question, not a torsion-reduction one.
