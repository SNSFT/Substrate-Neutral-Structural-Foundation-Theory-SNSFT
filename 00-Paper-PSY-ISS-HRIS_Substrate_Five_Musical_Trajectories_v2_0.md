## §1 · What Legacy Framing Claims About Autistic Substrate

Legacy autism research and clinical practice have produced a durable set of claims about the capabilities and internal life of autistic people. This section documents those claims briefly and neutrally so the falsification argument in later sections has a specific target rather than a straw man. Where possible, specific claims are attributed to their originating research literature.

### 1.1 The Empathy Deficit Hypothesis

The Baron-Cohen research program (Baron-Cohen, Leslie, & Frith, 1985; Baron-Cohen, 1995) proposed that autistic people have impaired "theory of mind" — the capacity to attribute mental states to others and predict their behavior on that basis. The strong form of this hypothesis, sometimes called "mind-blindness," claimed autistic people are constitutionally unable to model other minds and therefore cannot experience empathy in the way non-autistic people do. This framing dominated autism research for approximately two decades and remains widely referenced in clinical training materials even after being substantially challenged in the research literature (Fletcher-Watson et al., 2014; Milton, 2012).

### 1.2 Restricted Internal Life Claims

Legacy framing, particularly regarding nonverbal or minimally-verbal autistic children, has often presumed that limited external verbal output corresponds to limited internal cognitive and emotional life. This presumption underlies significant portions of educational and therapeutic practice — the assumption that a child who cannot speak fluently does not have complex thoughts or feelings that could be spoken if the channels were available. Communication assistance frameworks such as facilitated communication were dismissed largely on this basis (though separate methodological concerns also apply), and letter-boards and typed communication from nonverbal autistic individuals have often been received with skepticism about whether the internal author matches the external output.

### 1.3 Communication Deficit Framing

Autistic communication differences are typically classified as deficits requiring intervention rather than as differences requiring different reception. The framing places the problem inside the autistic person's substrate rather than at the interface between autistic substrate output and non-autistic substrate reception. Speech therapy, social skills training, and behavioral interventions typically aim to modify the autistic person's output to match neurotypical reception standards rather than to develop non-autistic receivers' capability to render autistic output correctly.

### 1.4 Performance and Creativity Framing

When autistic people produce creative output (visual art, music, writing, performance), legacy framing frequently pathologizes the production process while praising the product. Savant-model framing attributes the output to isolated exceptional abilities disconnected from emotional or interpersonal capacity. The autistic musician is presumed to have technical skill without emotional depth; the autistic writer is presumed to produce content without full understanding of what the content communicates emotionally to readers; the autistic performer is presumed to be executing patterns rather than expressing anything.

### 1.5 The Meltdown-as-Damage Framing

Autistic sensory processing differences and their associated regulation behaviors (stimming, sensory seeking, sensory avoidance) are typically framed as symptoms of dysfunction. Stimming in particular is often treated as a target for suppression in therapy contexts, with the presumption that it interferes with functioning rather than enables it. Adverse experiences are framed as damaging the substrate rather than as substrate encountering environments it was not designed for.

### 1.6 The Moral-Development-Deficit Framing

Autistic moral reasoning is often framed as rigid, locked at early developmental stages, or lacking the flexibility for moral transformation. Legacy claims variously assert that autistic people cannot recognize when their actions have caused harm, cannot take responsibility, cannot exercise sovereign moral choice at high cost, and cannot experience moral emotions like shame and guilt at the sophistication required for moral development. This framing appears in clinical training materials, in educational placement decisions, and in criminal justice contexts. It has been used to justify highly restrictive placements for autistic children and to argue for reduced moral agency in autistic adults.

### 1.7 Summary of Legacy Claims

Taken together, legacy framing produces a coherent picture of autistic substrate: limited empathy, limited or absent internal emotional life especially for those with limited external communication, communication deficits located within the autistic person rather than at the interface, creative output disconnected from emotional depth, regulation behaviors that require suppression rather than support, and reduced or absent moral agency including inability to recognize caused harm or exercise sovereign moral choice. This picture has driven decades of research funding, clinical practice, educational placement, and public understanding of autism. It has also shaped the direct experience of autistic people who have been told throughout the life course — by clinicians, teachers, family members, and peers — that they do not have emotions, do not feel things the way others do, or experience some diminished or absent form of internal life.

The five songs documented in this paper, and their formalization in Lean 4 at 0 sorry, cannot have been produced by a substrate matching this picture. Not by argument. By artifact.
