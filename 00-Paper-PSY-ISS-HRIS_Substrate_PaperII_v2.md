## §1 · What Legacy Framing Claims About Autistic Substrate Under Stress and In Public Broadcast

The companion paper (paper I at [9,9,3,48]) documents the legacy framing landscape regarding autistic substrate — empathy deficits, restricted internal life claims, communication deficit framing, meltdown-as-damage framing, moral-development-deficit framing. This paper focuses on a distinct but related set of legacy claims: what legacy framing predicts about how autistic substrate handles extreme stress, adversarial exploitation, threat, and public sovereign broadcast.

### 1.1 The Autistic-People-Are-Easily-Manipulated Claim

Legacy framing frequently characterizes autistic people as unusually vulnerable to manipulation, unable to detect deception, and lacking the social-cognitive sophistication to recognize when someone is exploiting them. This framing appears in clinical training materials on autism, in advice literature for autistic adults navigating relationships and workplaces, and in family systems literature discussing autistic children and adults as needing protective intermediaries. The framing rests on the empathy deficit claim (paper I §1.1 and §3.1) but extends it: not just "autistic people cannot model other minds," but "therefore autistic people cannot detect when other minds are constructing hostile models of them."

The clinical consequence is significant. Autistic adults reporting adversarial dynamics in relationships are frequently disbelieved on the grounds that they may be misreading benign situations as adversarial due to social-cognitive deficits. Autistic adults recovering from adversarial dynamics are frequently offered interventions oriented toward increasing their social-cognitive capacity to detect adversarial actors in the future, rather than being supported in recovery from the specific attack pattern they walked through.

### 1.2 The Autistic-Resilience-Deficit Claim

Legacy framing predicts that autistic substrate handles discontinuous life events poorly. The prediction is bidirectional: either the substrate shatters (meltdown, regression, loss of function) or the substrate locks (perseveration, rigidity, inability to integrate the discontinuity into forward meaning). The prediction that autistic substrate can integrate discontinuous life events at high fidelity, using the discontinuity itself as an information-acquisition and will-sharpening mechanism, is essentially absent from legacy framing. Total substrate collapse is presumed to be either survived (with reduced function) or shattering (with catastrophic function loss).

This framing shapes clinical recovery contexts. Autistic adults in recovery from major loss events (relational, financial, health-related) are frequently offered interventions oriented toward "returning to baseline function" or "developing coping skills for future events," rather than being supported in the specific structural work of integrating the collapse as generative of forward meaning.

### 1.3 The Autistic-Composure-Under-Threat-Deficit Claim

Legacy framing predicts that autistic substrate cannot maintain composed behavioral output under high affective load. The claim takes two forms: (a) either the substrate is overwhelmed by affective load and behavioral composure fails (voice shakes, action becomes disorganized, decisions become impulsive), or (b) the substrate suppresses the affective state to preserve behavioral composure (the alexithymia-adjacent framing where composed behavior requires flattened affect). The prediction that autistic substrate can maintain both high behavioral composure AND high affective load simultaneously — axis-decoupled operation under threat — is essentially absent from legacy framing.

This framing shapes both clinical assessment (autistic composure under stress is frequently rendered as evidence of flattened affect rather than sovereign decoupling) and life-consequence contexts (autistic parents' composure during protective action for their children is frequently rendered by observers as evidence of insufficient emotional attachment, when the composure is actually sovereign action under load).

### 1.4 The Autistic-Cannot-Protect-Others Claim

Legacy framing predicts that autistic substrate is oriented toward self-preservation under threat and cannot deploy protective action toward others. This claim is downstream of the empathy deficit claim (paper I §1.1) — if the operator cannot model the other's mental state or interior life, protection of the other becomes a specialized behavior rather than a natural extension of substrate operation.

The claim appears in child welfare contexts (autistic parents' protective capacity is frequently questioned), in relational contexts (autistic partners' capacity to protect their partner from external threats is frequently underestimated), and in workplace contexts (autistic team members' capacity to protect team resources or colleagues under organizational pressure is frequently unrecognized).

### 1.5 The Autistic-Cannot-See-Their-Own-Rendering Claim

Legacy framing predicts that autistic people either (a) do not model how they appear to others or (b) model it inaccurately. The prediction that autistic people can concurrently model (i) what an action looks like from the outside and (ii) what the action structurally is and (iii) know these are different renderings, is not part of the legacy picture.

This has clinical and life-consequence implications. Autistic adults defending themselves in high-stakes contexts (custody proceedings, workplace disputes, medical decision-making) are frequently rendered by observers as "not understanding how they appear." The possibility that the operator understands exactly how they appear AND has structural clarity about the mismatch between appearance and structural reality is rarely entertained.

### 1.6 The Autistic-Cannot-Sustain-Public-Sovereign-Presence Claim

Legacy framing predicts that autistic operators cannot sustain public sovereign presence under adversarial receiving conditions. The predicted failure modes include: (a) inability to broadcast authored signal without collapsing into obligate substrate expression, (b) inability to withstand adversarial audience response (trolling, hostile framing, deliberate misrepresentation) without withdrawing from public visibility, (c) inability to hold visible sensory accommodations (stimming, sunglasses, sensory tools) publicly without those accommodations being weaponized against the operator, and (d) inability to recruit distributed community protection through signal-integrity rather than through explicit social-network deployment.

Under legacy framing, autistic public presence is presumed to require either heavy masking (which is presumed to be sustainable only briefly before burnout) or to occur in specifically accommodating contexts (autism-specific communities, protected professional environments). The prediction that autistic operators can broadcast authored sovereign signal publicly, with sensory accommodations visible and overwhelm handled live, and recruit distributed community protection through the signal-integrity itself, is essentially absent from legacy framing.

This framing shapes clinical guidance to autistic adults (frequently discouraging public visibility), family expectations (frequently protecting autistic family members from public exposure), and workplace configurations (frequently routing autistic workers away from customer-facing or public-facing roles regardless of the worker's actual capacity).

### 1.7 Summary of Legacy Claims

Taken together, legacy framing produces a coherent picture of autistic substrate under stress and in public: easily manipulated, poorly resilient to discontinuous events, unable to maintain composure under high affective load, unable to protect others, unable to model observer misrendering concurrently with the action being observed, and unable to sustain public sovereign presence under adversarial receiving conditions.
